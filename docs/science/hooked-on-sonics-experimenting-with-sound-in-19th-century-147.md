summary: In the 19th century, popular science books democratized acoustic experimentation
  by presenting sound studies as entertaining, accessible activities for middle-class
  families and children. Building on Ernst Chladni's revolutionary 1777 experiment
  (which made sound visible through vibrating sand patterns), these primers encouraged
  home-based scientific discovery by blending rigorous learning with beauty and pleasure
  rather than detachment. This period of heightened "acoustic awareness" reflected
  broader Victorian fascination with sound and listening, establishing a progressive
  educational model that positioned science as playful intellectual discovery available
  to everyone.
