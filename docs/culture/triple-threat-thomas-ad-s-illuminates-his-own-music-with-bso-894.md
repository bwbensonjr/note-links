Triple threat Thomas Adès illuminates his own music with BSO
============================================================

For better and worse, no one conducts the music of Adès as well as Adès does himself.

By A.Z. Madonna
|
March 4th, 2026, 3:41 am

Globe Staff

Music Review

*BOSTON SYMPHONY ORCHESTRA*

At Symphony Hall Feb. 26. www.bso.org

Thomas Adès, one of the BSO’s most frequent collaborators in the past decade, is a rare triple threat of classical music. He composes, he conducts, he plays the piano with collaborators including tenor Ian Bostridge, pianist Kirill Gerstein, and the Boston Symphony Chamber Players. In between all that, he still finds time to do things like, say, give Paris Hilton and Nicole Richie a crash course on opera for their “Simple Life’’ reunion special. (Not kidding.)

This kind of do-it-all-ism was far more common in the 18th and 19th centuries (example: Beethoven did all three) than it is now. The modern paradigm of musical education tends to prioritize depth over breadth, so when performers get on the podium and/or conductors try their hands at composition, the results are sometimes perfunctory, sometimes heartwarming, but rarely memorable. This is not the case for Adès, who returned to Symphony Hall this week to lead a program of two of his own pieces plus Beethoven’s Symphony No. 6, “Pastoral.’’ His conducting, composing, and performing are clearly branches on the same tree.

For better and worse, no one conducts the music of Adès as well as Adès does himself. His pieces for orchestra tend to be monumental in vision even when they’re on the shorter side; the one-movement symphony “Tevot’’ is a good example of this. But Adès’s music only conveys that dizzying, humbling sense of scale when the conductor and musicians have a granular understanding of the moment-to-moment shifts in balance, dynamics, and texture that combine to create that sense of dimension. If you ask me, that’s why his music can be divisive; for some listeners it soars, but others wonder “What’s the big deal?’’ I might wonder that, too, if I had heard the slow central section of “Tevot’’ without that clear vision behind it and it failed to give me the same sensation I felt when first I stared into the Milky Way. Without the background details, it is slightly but palpably diminished. The difference isn’t precisely night and day, but it’s the difference between stargazing in rural Maine and doing it in downtown Boston.

So Adès was without question the right conductor to introduce his 2025 orchestral piece, “Aquifer,’’ to the BSO and Boston audiences. An aquifer is an underground body of rock or earth saturated with water, and the piece wore its elemental inspiration proudly; broad swaths of strings flowed through craggy formations of brass, and a bass drum thumped as if warning of an imminent eruption. Though the piece wasn’t straightforwardly melodic, it maintained a sense of constant motion in swells and troughs, ending with a startling, almost gaudy C major chord. Was the final destination of the aquifer’s water the Bellagio?

Augustin Hadelich, the BSO’s 2025-26 artist in residence, joined for the second Adès piece on the program, the violin concerto “Concentric Paths,’’ which turned out to be another case of dream casting. That concerto needs a soloist who is willing to pivot between ensemble player and soloist. The first movement was almost entirely orchestra-forward while Hadelich’s solo violin sailed overhead, sounding more like a bird whistle than most violins I’ve ever heard.

Arresting silences punctuated the dialogue between the solo violin’s aggressive quadruple stops and the responding dulcet brass chords; later, Hadelich’s keen lines arched into low earth orbit and back down while the low brass and strings roiled like molten rock below, and orchestra and soloist at last met on the same plane with a hypnotic lament. Hadelich is known to be fond of a unique encore, and he ended the first half with a playful spin through Coleridge-Taylor Perkinson’s “Louisiana Blues Strut,’’ which he recorded for his 2024 “American Road Trip’’ album.

That sense of play lingered through Beethoven’s Symphony No. 6, “Pastoral,’’ after intermission. The BSO can perform this piece in its sleep, but with Adès at the helm, sleepy would be the last word to describe it. He seemed to clip the last milliseconds of some phrases in the first movement, the better to get across the anticipation and excitement of arriving in the countryside. Beethoven was well into adulthood when he wrote the “Pastoral,’’ but summer can make children of us all, and the musical image of summer Adès conveyed was that of a child who had to be told he must not unbuckle his seat belt or, God forbid, open the door, until the family car had come to a complete stop. The luxuriant final movement typically lands like a sigh of grateful relief and contentment. In Adès’s hands Thursday evening, it danced.
