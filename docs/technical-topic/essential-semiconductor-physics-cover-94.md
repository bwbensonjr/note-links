The lectures are not about semiconductor devices; instead, they are about the under- lying physics that makes semiconductor devices possible. These lectures present an electrical engineering perspective, but those in other ﬁelds may ﬁnd it a useful introduction to the approach that has guided the development of semiconductor technology for more than 75 years. Before we continue, a brief word about units, which sometimes causes confusion. When we say the thermal energy is 3kBT/2, we often quote the numerical value in electron volts (eV), but remember that electron volt is not a legitimate system international (SI) unit. It’s a convenient unit to express energies in, but it’s not a proper unit for doing calculations. When computing the average energy, we should take 3/2 times Boltz- mann’s constant, 1.38 × 10−23 J/K times the temperature, T, in Kelvin, 300 K. The answer is in Joules. The average thermal energy is 6.21×10−21 Joules. That’s not a convenient number to remember. It is more conve- nient to express these energies in electron volts. To convert an energy in Joules to electron volts, divide the energy in Joules by the magnitude of the charge on an electron, q = 1.6 × 10−19 C, and get 0.039 eV. To use this energy in a formula with SI units, we need to convert back into Joules and insert that into the formula. An electron volt is not a proper SI unit, so be careful about that when you are working out semiconductor problems. At 300 K, there is thermal energy, which is proportional to Boltzmann’s constant times temperature. The silicon atoms jiggle around in random thermal motion with an average kinetic energy of 3kBT/2. Since there is thermal energy, there is a small probability that some valence bonds will be broken. When that happens, there are empty states in the valence band, just a few. They go to the conduction band. For a typical semiconductor at room temperature, only a tiny fraction of the large number of valence band states are empty. There is a large number of empty states in the conduction band, and a tiny fraction of them are ﬁlled with electrons. The valence band and conduction bands, are separated by the forbidden gap, or band gap, EG. A broken bond produces a hole, an empty state in the valence band, and an electron, a ﬁlled state in the conduction band, so the number of holes and electrons is equal. This important number is the intrinsic carrier concentration. Electrons in the conduction band are mobile and conduct electrical currents. Holes in the valence band are also mobile and conduct electrical currents. The particle is called a photon. The energy of a photon is E = hf, Planck’s constant, h, times the frequency of the light. If the frequency is high, or the wavelength short (e.g. blue or violet or ultraviolet) then the photons will have a lot of energy, E ≫EG, and can lift an electron deep in the valence band way up into the conduction band creating very energetic electrons in the conduction band, and energetic holes deep in the valence band. We call these carriers hot carriers (hot electrons and hot holes) because temperature is a way of measuring the kinetic energy of a carrier. Electrons are typically near the bottom of the conduction band, and holes are near the top of the valence band. This happens because very quickly (within femtoseconds) hot electrons lose energy and drops down to the bottom of the conduction band. In the valence band, electrons above the hot hole drop down and ﬁll the hole, so the hole moves up in energy, and within a few femtoseconds it is near the top of the valence band. We will assume in these lectures that electrons are always near EC and holes are near EV and will ignore processes that occur on very short time scales. The most important concept introduced in this lecture is that for isolated silicon atoms, there is a set of discrete energy levels, but in semiconductor crystals, the electrons interact with each other, and the energy levels broaden and become bands of energies, energy bands. Because everything typically happens in near the top of the valence band and near the bottom of the conduction band, those are the two regions of energy that we will focus on in these lectures.
