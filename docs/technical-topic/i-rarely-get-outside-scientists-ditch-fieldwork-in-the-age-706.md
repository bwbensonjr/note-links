summary: Scientists increasingly rely on AI, digitized specimens, sensors, and remote
  data analysis rather than traditional fieldwork to study ecology, enabling unprecedented
  monitoring of biodiversity at scales previously impossible. While this technological
  revolution offers significant potential for understanding ecological crises and
  global patterns, some ecologists worry that the shift away from hands-on fieldwork
  risks losing intimate knowledge of nature, introducing bias and oversimplification,
  and reducing engagement with local communities essential for conservation. The debate
  reflects a fundamental tension between the efficiency and scale of data-driven science
  and the irreplaceable insights gained from direct experience with the natural world.
