# Opinion | AI's Claude is ubiquitous. Check out its brilliant namesake..

If you managed to keep your eyes open — and your tear ducts closed — during the Super Bowl Sunday night, you would have [seen an ad](https://seekingalpha.com/news/4549009-anthropics-claude-tops-openai-in-super-bowl-ad-engagement-edo-ceo-krim-says---cnbc-interview) for Anthropic’s artificial intelligence “assistant” named Claude.

Claude is suddenly ubiquitous: “[Anthropic’s breakout](https://www.ft.com/content/a75555a6-24c3-4468-aba9-7fe12b5def31) moment: how Claude won business and shook markets” (Financial Times). “[Claude Is Taking](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/anthropic-claude-code-ai-7a46460e?gaa_at=eafs&gaa_n=AWEtsqe15a6cPDN52U-uXB32rk07WIt1vpwPQE3mmVgeBuq8sZ4sGE4mNQNcpf6nCJ4%3D&gaa_ts=6989f543&gaa_sig=8R0KYROoAR9Eio9XC4oIZVfm2S4t39gxFmi-yhkvOOfyNfiVmSgmMn6qDtYiFPwazgxZH-oWBKHMEGcti5nLFg%3D%3D) the AI World by Storm, and Even Non-Nerds Are Blown Away” (The Wall Street Journal). And so on.

I learned only recently that Claude is an homage to the late MIT professor Claude Shannon, who lived on Mystic Lake in Winchester for more than 40 years. He died in 2001 at 84. Shannon, whom I first encountered in Jon Gertner’s informative 2012 book “The Idea Factory: Bell Labs and the Great Age of American Innovation,” became known as “the Father of the Information Age.”

“It’s Shannon whose fingerprints are on every electronic device we own, every computer screen we gaze into, every means of digital communication,” author James Gleick, author of “The Information,” told [The New Yorker](https://www.newyorker.com/tech/annals-of-technology/claude-shannon-the-father-of-the-information-age-turns-1100100) in 2016, on Shannon’s centenary. “He’s one of these people who so transform the world that, after the transformation, the old world is forgotten.”

But don’t hold that against him — Shannon was one incredible fellow!

In a Reddit [Ask me Anything](https://www.reddit.com/r/IAmA/comments/6pa11p/we_spent_5_years_studying_claude_shannonthe/) session nine years ago, Shannon’s biographers Rob Goodman and Jimmy Soni described their subject as “a cross between Albert Einstein and the Dos Equis guy.” (They relate a famous anecdote about Einstein wandering into one of Shannon’s lectures at Princeton’s Institute for Advanced Study and whispering to a student at the back of the room. Apparently, Einstein was seeking directions to the bathroom.)

Shannon’s Winchester house had a two-story “toy room” filled with contraptions he made. Among the toys: “one of the world’s earliest chess-playing computers, a mouse that could solve a maze (an early example of artificial intelligence), and … the world’s first wearable device (a machine to get better odds at roulette).”

Their biography recounts how Shannon and Ed Thorp, a junior professor at MIT, traveled to Las Vegas [in 1961](https://www.cs.virginia.edu/~evans/thorp.pdf) with their wives to test a cigarette pack-sized device controlled by their toes “with microswitches in [their] shoes,” which chirped out musical tones corresponding to the eight sectors — imagine a sliced pizza — of the roulette wheel.

Fearful of casino goons on the lookout for gamers seeking to improve their odds, the couples chickened out after one trial run. “The wearable computer was consigned to Shannon’s growing heap of curiosities,” the authors write.

These examples barely fill a small shelf in Shannon’s crowded cabinet of curiosities. For instance, he was obsessed with juggling and famously unicycled down the corridors at Bell Labs while juggling several balls. He once hung an MIT student juggler upside down in his living room to test the inverted physics of “bounce juggling,” using the ceiling as a floor. “It was a complete failure,” the student, Arthur Lewbel, told Shannon’s biographers.

There’s more. The New Yorker [tribute](https://www.newyorker.com/tech/annals-of-technology/claude-shannon-the-father-of-the-information-age-turns-1100100) reported that “Shannon built a machine that did arithmetic with Roman numerals, naming it THROBAC, for Thrifty Roman-Numeral Backward-Looking Computer. He built a flame-throwing trumpet and a rocket-powered Frisbee.”

Shannon also designed what he called The Ultimate Machine, [a box with a switch](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=G5rJJgt_5mg) that activates an artificial hand that emerges from the box to turn itself off. To say nothing of [the poem he submitted](https://www.scientificamerican.com/blog/cross-check/poetic-masterpiece-of-claude-shannon-father-of-information-theory-published-for-the-first-time/) to Scientific American on Rubik’s Cube, which begins: “Strange imports come from Hungary / Count Dracula, and ZsaZsa G. / Now Erno Rubik’s Magic Cube / For PhD or country rube.”

So much for the storied professor Shannon. How did Anthropic’s AI Claude-bot get so smart? In part, by stealing information from 500,000 copyrighted books (including five of mine), resulting in a $1.5 billion [settlement award](https://www.npr.org/2025/09/05/nx-s1-5529404/anthropic-settlement-authors-copyright-ai) on behalf of the copyrighted authors.

Stealing information and hiring lawyers to (unsuccessfully) justify your egregious theft? That’s not something the real Claude would do.
